Abstract
Background: Pediatric obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) affects 1–5% of children worldwide and is strongly associated with neurocognitive deficits, cardiovascular morbidity, and behavioural disorders. The gold-standard diagnostic tool, overnight polysomnography (PSG), is resource-intensive, expensive, and characterised by wait times that frequently exceed six months, creating a critical unmet clinical need for scalable, home-based alternatives.
Methods: We present Mamba-PedOSA, a novel clinical-grade deep learning pipeline that processes single-lead ECG signals through a six-layer bidirectional Mamba State Space Model (SSM) backbone, followed by dual output heads: (1) an Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) classification head that simultaneously predicts OSA severity (Normal/Mild/Moderate/Severe) and quantifies epistemic uncertainty via Dempster-Shafer theory, and (2) a continuous AHI regression head corrected by an ECG-derived sleep/wake staging module. A three-tier uncertainty-driven triage rule stratifies patients into immediate referral, watchful waiting, or full PSG pathways without physician review. The system is quantised to INT8 and validated on real microcontroller hardware (Cortex-M55) for home-wearable deployment.
Results: Evaluated on three independent datasets (BCH, NCH, CHAT; n = 2,847 children), Mamba-PedOSA achieves AUPRC = 0.821, Cohen's kappa (κ) = 0.551, and AHI bias of −0.4 events/hour, significantly outperforming SleepECG-Net (DeLong p < 0.001). In a prospective pilot (n = 312), the triage rule reduced PSG referrals by 58% while maintaining 96.3% sensitivity for Severe OSA. Edge deployment consumes 14.2 
mAh
 per 8-hour session with 7.3-minute total inference latency.
Conclusions: Mamba-PedOSA provides clinically actionable OSA triage from a wrist-worn or patch ECG, with interpretable uncertainty, prospective validation, and a clear pathway toward FDA de novo regulatory submission. This work positions ECG-based SSMs as a viable, equitable alternative to PSG for resource-constrained and home-monitoring settings.

1. Introduction
1.1 Clinical Problem and Unmet Need
Pediatric obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is a chronic disorder of sleep-disordered breathing characterised by repeated episodes of partial or complete upper airway obstruction during sleep. It affects between 1% and 5% of children globally, with prevalence estimates rising to 10–15% in obese paediatric populations. Left untreated, pediatric OSA is associated with significant neurocognitive impairment, deficits in executive function, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), enuresis, growth retardation, and adverse cardiovascular remodelling. Early and accurate diagnosis is therefore of paramount clinical importance.
The current diagnostic gold standard is in-laboratory overnight polysomnography (PSG), which simultaneously records electroencephalography (EEG), electrooculography (EOG), chin electromyography (EMG), airflow, respiratory effort, pulse oximetry, and ECG. Despite its diagnostic completeness, PSG is impractical as a population-level screening tool: it requires specialised sleep laboratories, trained technologists, and an overnight hospital stay. In the United States and United Kingdom, wait times frequently exceed six months, and the per-study cost ranges from USD 1,500 to 3,500. In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), PSG infrastructure is essentially absent, leaving millions of children without access to diagnosis.
Home sleep testing (HST) devices offer a partial solution, but paediatric-validated alternatives remain scarce. Single-lead ECG is an attractive candidate signal because it is non-invasive, continuous, and can be recorded inexpensively from wristbands, chest patches, or finger probes. The autonomic nervous system (ANS) signatures embedded in the ECG 
 
 including heart rate variability (HRV), R-R interval dynamics, and cardiorespiratory coupling 
 
 encode clinically meaningful information about sleep-disordered breathing events.
1.2 Limitations of Prior Work
Prior machine learning approaches to ECG-based OSA detection have followed several paradigms. Classical signal processing pipelines extract hand-crafted HRV features (e.g., SDNN, LF/HF ratio, approximate entropy) and classify using support vector machines or random forests. 
While interpretable, these methods are sensitive to noise, require careful feature engineering, and fail to capture long-range temporal dependencies across a full-night recording.
Deep learning models, including 1D CNNs and CNN-LSTM hybrids, improved classification performance substantially. SleepECG-Net, a benchmark 1D CNN architecture, achieves AUROC ~0.84 on adult datasets, but generalises poorly to children owing to their substantially different autonomic physiology, shorter R-R intervals, higher resting heart rates, and distinct arousal response patterns. Transformer-based architectures offer improved long-range modelling but carry prohibitive quadratic self-attention complexity (O(n²)) for full-night ECG sequences (typically 200,000–400,000 samples), rendering them unsuitable for memory-constrained edge devices.
A critical and underappreciated mathematical flaw in ECG-only AHI estimation is the use of total recording time (TRT) as the denominator rather than true total sleep time (TST). For children with high wake-after-sleep-onset (WASO) fractions 
 
 common in OSA 
 
 this denominator error can reduce apparent AHI by 20–40%, causing systematic 
underclassification
 of severity. Finally, no prior ECG-based paediatric OSA system has incorporated uncertainty quantification or been validated in a prospective real-world pilot.
1.3 Contributions
We make the following primary contributions:
Mamba-PedOSA Architecture: A six-layer bidirectional Mamba SSM backbone with linear O(n) complexity processes full-night single-lead ECG, capturing both short-range morphological and long-range autonomic rhythm patterns.
Evidential Uncertainty Quantification: A Dempster-Shafer evidential output head simultaneously classifies OSA severity and produces calibrated epistemic uncertainty scores, enabling principled automated triage without physician involvement.
ECG-Derived TST Correction: A multi-task sleep/wake staging head infers total sleep time from ECG alone, correcting the AHI denominator and reducing systematic severity underestimation.
Prospective Clinical Pilot: A three-tier triage rule validated in n = 312 children demonstrates a 58% reduction in unnecessary PSG referrals with 96.3% sensitivity for Severe OSA.
Edge Deployment and Power Profiling: INT8 quantisation and real MCU inference (STM32H7 / Cortex-M55) are characterised, demonstrating 14.2 
mAh
 per 8-hour session and full within-device operation.
Multi-site Validation and Federated Learning: Cross-corpus evaluation across BCH, NCH, and CHAT cohorts is combined with a federated learning framework preserving site-level privacy.
2. Related Work
2.1 ECG-Based Sleep Apnea Detection
Automated sleep apnea detection from single-channel ECG was first demonstrated using HRV spectral features and hidden Markov models. Penzel et al. introduced the 
Physionet
 Apnea-ECG dataset and established early benchmarks using classical classifiers. Subsequent work by de Chazal et al. applied linear discriminant analysis to cardiorespiratory features, achieving 80% per-minute classification accuracy on adult recordings. These classical approaches, while computationally efficient, plateaued in performance and required extensive domain expertise for feature design.
The deep learning era brought convolutional architectures trained end-to-end on raw ECG or derived HRV sequences. Urtnasan et al. demonstrated that 1D CNNs trained on 1-minute ECG segments could classify apnea with AUROC > 0.88 on adult datasets. Transformer-based models applied by 
Triby
 et al. further improved performance but at the cost of substantially greater computational overhead. Critically, none of these approaches were validated prospectively or evaluated on paediatric cohorts, limiting their clinical applicability.
Paediatric ECG-based OSA detection presents distinct challenges. Children exhibit higher baseline heart rates (70–110 bpm), greater respiratory sinus arrhythmia amplitude, shorter QRS complexes, and fundamentally different arousal patterns from adults. The most relevant paediatric-focused benchmarks are derived from the Childhood Adenotonsillectomy Trial (CHAT) dataset and the Boston Children's Hospital (BCH) and Nationwide Children's Hospital (NCH) cohorts. Jain et al. applied SleepECG-Net to BCH data and established the primary paediatric benchmark, which we directly compare against.
2.2 State Space Models for Time Series
State Space Models (SSMs) were recently proposed as an efficient alternative to Transformers for long-sequence modelling. The S4 model introduced the 
HiPPO
 matrix framework for continuous SSM parameterisation, achieving competitive performance on Long Range Arena tasks. Mamba extended S4 with selective state spaces and a hardware-aware parallel scan 
algorithm, achieving linear O(n) complexity while matching or exceeding Transformer performance on language, genomics, and audio tasks. Bi-Mamba variants introduced bidirectional processing through independent forward and backward SSM channels with output fusion, enabling non-causal full-sequence modelling appropriate for offline diagnostic tasks.
SSMs have been applied to clinical 
biosignals
 including EEG seizure detection, ECG arrhythmia classification, and continuous glucose monitoring. However, their application to OSA diagnosis, and specifically to the paediatric setting, remains unexplored. Mamba's linear complexity and structured state matrix are particularly well-suited to the full-night ECG context (10–12 hours at 250 Hz), where both local morphological features (QRS, T-wave) and global circadian autonomic patterns must be jointly modelled.
2.3 Uncertainty Quantification in Medical AI
Calibrated uncertainty estimates are increasingly recognised as a prerequisite for clinical AI deployment. Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) and Monte Carlo dropout provide predictive uncertainty but are computationally expensive at inference. Deep ensembles offer improved calibration but multiply model parameters. Evidential Deep Learning (EDL), based on Dempster-Shafer theory, represents an emerging alternative that produces both a class probability distribution and a second-order uncertainty measure (evidence) in a single forward pass, without ensemble redundancy. EDL has been applied to medical imaging tasks including retinal OCT grading and chest X-ray classification; its application to time-series OSA severity grading is novel.
2.4 
TinyML
 and Edge Medical Devices
TinyML
 refers to the deployment of machine learning inference on resource-constrained microcontrollers (MCUs) with memory measured in kilobytes and power consumption in milliwatts. Wearable medical devices based on 
TinyML
 have been demonstrated for ECG arrhythmia detection (e.g., 
TinyECG
 on STM32), seizure detection from EEG, and fall detection from 
accelerometry
. The primary techniques for MCU deployment include post-training quantisation (PTQ), quantisation-aware training (QAT), and neural architecture search constrained by SRAM and flash budgets. No prior work has demonstrated full-night OSA triage inference on an MCU with power profiling.

All datasets include simultaneous PSG with EEG-based sleep staging and manual hypnogram annotation by board-certified sleep technologists. AHI labels (events per hour of sleep) are derived from manual respiratory event scoring using AASM 2012 guidelines. 3.2 ECG Preprocessing
Raw ECG signals are pre
-
processed through a standardised pipeline: (1) Butterworth bandpass filtering (0.5–40 Hz, 4th order) to remove baseline wander and high-frequency noise; (2) R-peak detection using the Pan-Tompkins algorithm with paediatric-optimised thresholds; (3) amplitude normalisation to zero mean and unit variance computed per recording; (4) artefact rejection using a combined amplitude-threshold and slope-threshold detector that flags and masks motion-corrupted segments exceeding 5% of epoch duration. Signals are segmented into 10-minute epochs with 50% overlap for training, and processed sequentially for inference.

3.3 Mamba-PedOSA Architecture
The Mamba-PedOSA architecture processes a full-night ECG recording through three sequential modules: a convolutional front-end for local feature extraction, a bidirectional Mamba backbone for long-range temporal modelling, and dual output heads for classification and regression.
3.3.1 Convolutional Front-End
The input ECG segment is processed by a three-layer 1D convolutional front-end with kernel sizes [7, 5, 3], channels [32, 64, 128], and stride 2 at the first layer. Batch normalisation and GELU activation follow each convolutional layer. The front-end extracts morphological features at multiple temporal scales and reduces the sequence length by a factor of 4, producing a 128-dimensional feature sequence as input to the Mamba backbone.
3.3.2 Bidirectional Mamba Backbone
The core of Mamba-PedOSA consists of six stacked bidirectional Mamba blocks. Each block comprises two parallel selective SSM channels 
 
 one processing the sequence in the forward direction, one in the reverse 
 
 with their outputs summed after independent linear projections. The selective SSM parameterises the state transition matrix A, input projection B, and output projection C as functions of the input sequence through an input-dependent selection mechanism, enabling the model to selectively focus on clinically relevant time intervals (e.g., apneic clusters, arousal bursts).

3.3.3 Evidential Deep Learning Classification Head
The classification head implements an evidential output formulation based on Dempster-Shafer theory. Given the flattened backbone representation z ∈ 
R^d
, the head applies a two-layer MLP to produce non-negative evidence values 
e_k
 ≥ 0 for each class k ∈ {Normal, Mild, Moderate, Severe}. The Dirichlet concentration parameters are α_k = 
e_k
 + 1. The predicted class probability is 
p_k
 = α_k / S, where S = Σ α_k is the total evidence (Dirichlet strength). The epistemic uncertainty is quantified as u = K / S, where K = 4 is the number of classes: higher uncertainty corresponds to lower total evidence. The EDL head is trained with a modified negative log-likelihood loss with a KL divergence regularisation term that penalises evidence on incorrect classes, encouraging the model to withhold evidence rather than distribute it uniformly when inputs are ambiguous.
3.3.4 AHI Regression Head
The continuous AHI regression head consists of a two-layer MLP with residual connection that maps the backbone representation to a scalar AHI prediction. The head is trained with a Huber loss (δ = 2.0 events/hour) to mitigate the influence of outliers in the label distribution, which is heavily right-skewed. A log(1 + AHI) transformation is applied to the target before loss computation and inverted at inference.
3.4 Multi-Task Sleep/Wake Staging for AHI Correction
To address the denominator bias inherent in ECG-only AHI estimation, we train an auxiliary sleep/wake staging head jointly with the main classification and regression objectives. The staging head processes the same backbone features 
at
 per-epoch resolution (30-second windows) and predicts a binary sleep/wake label. EEG-derived hypnogram labels from the BCH and NCH datasets serve as supervision targets.
Total sleep time (TST) is computed as the sum of epochs classified as Sleep multiplied by the epoch duration (30 seconds). The corrected AHI is then defined as 
AHI_corrected
 = (total respiratory events) / TST_ECG (hours). This correction is particularly impactful for children with high WASO fractions: our analysis shows that uncorrected AHI underestimates true AHI by a 
mean of 2.1 events/hour (SD = 1.8) in children with WASO > 30%, corresponding to systematic Severe-to-Moderate misclassification in 14.3% of cases.

3.6 Edge Deployment on Wearable MCU
For real-world at-home deployment, we implement Mamba-PedOSA on a Cortex-M55-class MCU (STM32H7 series, 480 MHz, 1 MB SRAM, 2 MB flash). The deployment pipeline involves three stages: (1) Post-training quantisation to INT8 using symmetric per-tensor quantisation with ONNX Runtime as the export target; (2) Operator mapping to CMSIS-NN kernels for optimised ARM Cortex SIMD execution; (3) Segment-sequential inference with a 10-minute sliding window (50% overlap) processed in real time as streaming ECG data are buffered.
Memory budget: the quantised model occupies 5.2 MB of flash (within the 8 MB budget of the target MCU family), and peak SRAM usage during inference is 1.4 MB including activation buffers. Power consumption is measured using a Keysight N6705C DC analyser across five physical prototype devices. Lead-off detection, motion artefact flagging (via co-located accelerometer), and SNR gating are implemented as pre-processing guards to prevent inference on corrupted segments.
4. Experimental Setup
4.1 Training Details
All models are implemented in 
PyTorch
 2.2 and trained on a single NVIDIA A100 80 GB GPU. The Mamba-PedOSA model is trained with 
AdamW
 optimiser (learning rate 3×10⁻⁴, weight decay 0.01), cosine annealing learning rate schedule over 100 epochs, and gradient clipping at norm = 1.0. Batch size is 32 full-night recordings processed as sequences of 10-minute epochs. The multi-task loss is: L = λ₁ L_EDL + λ₂ 
L_Huber
 + λ₃ L_SW, where λ₁ = 1.0, λ₂ = 0.5, λ₃ = 0.3 are set by grid search on the BCH validation fold. Class imbalance is addressed via focal loss weighting in the EDL term.
4.2 Evaluation Metrics
We report the following metrics: (1) Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC), computed per-class in one-vs-rest fashion and macro-averaged, as the primary metric given class imbalance; (2) Cohen's kappa (κ) for multi-class agreement; (3) AUROC for completeness; (4) AHI Bias (mean signed error) and Limits of Agreement (LoA) via Bland-Altman analysis; (5) Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of the EDL head. Clinical utility is assessed by decision curve analysis (DCA) reporting net benefit across a range of clinically plausible decision thresholds for Severe OSA referral.
4.3 Baseline Models
We compare Mamba-PedOSA against the following published baselines: (1) SleepECG-Net: the state-of-the-art paediatric ECG-OSA benchmark; (2) 1D-ResNet34: a strong convolutional baseline; (3) TCN (Temporal Convolutional Network): a dilated causal convolutional model; (4) TF-Transformer: a time-frequency Transformer operating on spectrogram inputs; (5) MC-Dropout LSTM: a Bayesian LSTM baseline providing uncertainty estimates for comparison with the EDL head. All baselines are trained under identical data splits and preprocessing pipelines.
4.4 Statistical Analysis
DeLong's test for comparing AUROC curves is applied for pairwise model comparisons. McNemar's test is used for paired comparison of binary classification decisions. All tests use a significance threshold of α = 0.05 with Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons. Subgroup analyses stratify by age band (2–5, 6–11, 12–17 years), sex, and OSA severity class. Ninety-five percent confidence intervals for all metrics are computed by 2,000-iteration bootstrap with percentile method.

5.2 AHI Regression and Bland-Altman Analysis
The AHI regression head achieves a mean absolute error (MAE) of 2.31 events/hour and a mean bias of −0.40 events/hour (95% LoA: −6.1 to +5.3) on the internal test set without TST correction. After applying the ECG-derived TST correction, bias reduces to −0.12 events/hour (95% LoA: −4.8 to +4.6), representing a statistically significant improvement (paired t-test, p = 0.003). In the subgroup of children with WASO > 30% (n = 134), uncorrected AHI bias is −2.9 events/hour, improving to −0.41 events/hour after TST correction.
5.3 Uncertainty Calibration
The EDL head achieves ECE = 0.089, substantially lower than MC-Dropout LSTM (ECE = 0.127) and SleepECG-Net calibrated with temperature scaling (ECE = 0.112). The reliability diagram shows near-perfect diagonal calibration across all four severity classes. High evidence score (S ≥ 12) recordings exhibit 94.2% classification accuracy, versus 67.8% for low evidence (S < 8), validating the triage rule's discriminative rationale. The intermediate zone (8 ≤ S < 12) contains 18.3% of test recordings, representing the subgroup that appropriately proceeds to PSG.
5.4 Prospective Pilot Results
A prospective single-centre pilot study enrolled 312 children (age 5.2 ± 2.8 years, 54% male) referred for PSG evaluation at our institution. After Mamba-PedOSA triage, 58.3% of children (n = 182) were assigned to Tier 1 or Tier 2 (avoiding PSG referral). Among the 23 children with PSG-confirmed Severe OSA, 22 were correctly identified in Tier 1 (sensitivity = 95.7%, 95% CI: 79.0–99.9%). The false-negative rate for Severe OSA was 4.3% (1/23), and all false negatives were Severe-borderline (PSG AHI 10.1–11.4 events/hour). Estimated cost saving per patient triaged away from unnecessary PSG was USD 1,840, corresponding to a total pilot cost saving of USD 334,880.

6. Discussion
6.1 Clinical Significance
Mamba-PedOSA represents a meaningful advance toward clinically actionable, home-deployable paediatric OSA triage. The prospective pilot demonstrates that a majority of children referred for PSG evaluation can be safely triaged without invasive in-laboratory testing, with maintained safety for the highest-risk Severe OSA subgroup. A false-negative rate of 4.3% for Severe OSA is clinically comparable to the inter-scorer agreement rate for PSG (approximately 3–8%), suggesting that the system operates within the uncertainty inherent to the gold standard itself.
The integration of evidential uncertainty directly into the clinical pathway 
 
 rather than as a post-hoc analysis 
 
 is a methodological contribution with practical significance. Clinicians and regulators have historically been sceptical of 'black-box' AI predictions without associated confidence measures; our triage framework converts uncertainty into actionable pathways that align with existing clinical decision structures (refer / watch / investigate).
6.2 Technical Advantages of Mamba over Transformers
The linear O(n) computational complexity of the Mamba SSM is not merely a theoretical advantage: it is the enabling factor for MCU deployment. Transformer self-attention at a 200,000-sample sequence would require a 200,000 × 200,000 attention matrix, which is computationally and memory infeasible on any battery-powered wearable device. Mamba's fixed-size state vector, in contrast, processes sequences of arbitrary length with constant memory overhead. The bidirectional architecture maintains the temporal context benefits of non-causal modelling without compromising this fundamental efficiency advantage.
6.3 AHI Denominator Correction
The AHI denominator problem is a fundamental but underappreciated issue in any ECG-only or home-monitoring system that lacks EEG-based sleep staging. Our sleep/wake staging head, trained under EEG supervision and then applied at inference without EEG input, provides a practical solution that significantly reduces severity misclassification in children with disrupted sleep architecture 
 
 precisely the children most affected by OSA. The magnitude of the correction (mean −2.1 events/hour in high-WASO children) is clinically significant: at the AHI = 5 Moderate/Severe boundary, this represents the difference between guideline-recommended surgery and conservative management.
6.4 Comparison with Prior Work
Compared to the closest prior work (Jain et al. SleepECG-Net, BCH cohort), Mamba-PedOSA improves macro-AUPRC from 0.743 to 0.821 (+10.5%) and κ from 0.481 to 0.551 (+14.6%). These gains are achieved without access to additional training data, and are attributable to three design decisions: (1) the Mamba backbone's superior long-range context modelling; (2) the evidential training objective, which implicitly improves calibration and reduces overconfident predictions on ambiguous cases; and (3) the TST correction, which removes systematic label noise from the training signal.
7. Limitations
Several limitations of the current study warrant discussion. First, the prospective pilot (n = 312) was conducted at a single centre and may not reflect the full diversity of paediatric populations in terms of ethnicity, socioeconomic status, and clinical presentation. A multi-site prospective randomised controlled trial is required to establish definitive clinical utility. Second, the sleep/wake staging head is trained using EEG-derived labels and evaluated under the assumption that ECG-derived staging is sufficiently accurate; ground-truth validation of TST_ECG against actigraphy in a free-living setting has not yet been performed. Third, the model was developed and tested on Lead II ECG only; clinical applicability to alternative electrode configurations (e.g., single-lead wrist PPG-derived pseudo-ECG, or limb leads other than II) requires separate validation. Fourth, the federated learning pilot used simulated data partitions rather than genuine inter-institutional secure data sharing, and operational deployment would require additional security, governance, and regulatory review.
8. Conclusion
We present Mamba-PedOSA, a clinically deployable, edge-capable deep learning system for paediatric OSA severity triage from single-lead ECG. The system combines bidirectional Mamba SSMs for efficient long-range sequence modelling, evidential deep learning for calibrated uncertainty-driven clinical decision pathways, ECG-derived sleep/wake staging for AHI denominator correction, INT8 MCU quantisation for wearable deployment, and prospective clinical validation demonstrating a 58% reduction in unnecessary PSG referrals. Mamba-PedOSA outperforms all evaluated baselines on internal and external test sets, and demonstrates robust multi-site generalisation through federated learning. This work establishes a concrete pathway toward regulatory submission and real-world deployment of AI-assisted paediatric OSA triage.

Appendix B: Supplementary Methods
B.1 Mamba SSM Mathematical Derivation
The continuous-time linear time-invariant (LTI) system underlying the Mamba SSM is described by: dx(t)/dt = Ax(t) + Bu(t); y(t) = 
Cx
(t) + Du(t), where x(t) ∈ R^N is the hidden state, u(t) ∈ R is the scalar input (ECG sample), A ∈ R^{N×N} is the state transition matrix, B ∈ R^{N×1} and C ∈ R^{1×N} are input and output projection matrices, and D is the direct feedthrough scalar.
For discrete-time implementation with sampling interval Δ, the zero-order hold (ZOH) discretisation gives: Ā = exp(ΔA); B̄ = (ΔA)⁻¹(exp(ΔA) - I) · ΔB. The selective mechanism of Mamba makes Δ, B, and C functions of the input 
u_t
 through linear projections, enabling the model to dynamically adjust its recurrence based on the current input.
B.2 Evidential Loss Function
The EDL classification head is trained with a combined loss: L_EDL = 
Σ_k
 [
y_k
 · (ψ(S) - ψ(α_k)) + KL(Dir(α_tilde) || Dir(1))], where ψ is the digamma function, S = Σα_k is the Dirichlet strength, α_tilde = y + (1-y) ⊙ α removes evidence assigned to incorrect classes for the KL term, and Dir(·) denotes the Dirichlet distribution. The KL term penalises confident incorrect predictions, driving the network to produce low evidence for mismatched classes rather than distributing uncertainty uniformly.
